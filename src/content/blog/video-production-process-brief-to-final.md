## The Short Answer

The professional video production process follows five core stages: Discovery and Brief, Concept and Pre-Production, Production Day, Post-Production, and Review and Delivery. Each stage builds on the last, transforming your initial idea into a polished final video. Understanding what happens at each phase helps you collaborate more effectively with your production team, avoid common delays, and get better results from your investment.

Whether you are commissioning your first corporate video or planning a large-scale brand campaign, this guide walks you through exactly what to expect at every step of the journey.

## Stage 1: Discovery and Brief

**Typical timeline: 1 to 5 days**

Every successful video starts with a conversation. The discovery stage is where we learn about your business, your goals, your audience, and your vision. This is the foundation that everything else is built upon, and getting it right saves significant time and money downstream.

### What Happens During Discovery

The discovery process usually begins with an initial consultation. This can be a face-to-face meeting, a video call, or even a detailed email exchange depending on the scope of the project. During this conversation, we explore several key areas.

**Your objectives.** What do you want this video to achieve? Are you trying to generate leads, explain a complex product, build brand awareness, recruit talent, or document an event? The objective shapes every creative decision that follows.

**Your audience.** Who will watch this video? A corporate training video for internal staff requires a very different approach than a social media ad targeting new customers. We dig into demographics, psychographics, and viewing context.

**Your message.** What is the single most important thing you want viewers to take away? Great videos communicate one clear message powerfully rather than trying to say everything at once.

**Your brand.** We review your existing brand guidelines, tone of voice, visual identity, and any previous video content. Consistency matters, even when you are trying something new.

**Your constraints.** Budget, timeline, locations, stakeholder approval processes, legal or compliance requirements, and distribution channels all inform the creative approach.

### What You Should Expect

After the discovery session, you will receive a project brief document that summarizes everything we discussed. This brief becomes the single source of truth for the project. You will have the opportunity to review it, make corrections, and add anything that was missed before we move forward.

You should also expect to receive a preliminary estimate or a recommendation on which of our [pricing packages](/pricing/) best fits your project scope.

### Tips to Make This Stage Smoother

- **Gather reference material before the meeting.** Links to videos you admire, competitor examples, or even videos you do not like are all incredibly useful. Showing is faster than telling.
- **Identify your decision-makers early.** Nothing derails a project faster than a new stakeholder appearing halfway through with a completely different vision. Make sure everyone with approval authority is part of the brief.
- **Be honest about your budget.** A good production company will tailor their approach to deliver the best possible result within your budget rather than upselling you on things you do not need.
- **Define your success metrics.** If you know how you will measure the video's performance, share that information. It directly influences creative strategy.

## Stage 2: Concept and Pre-Production

**Typical timeline: 3 to 10 days**

Pre-production is where the plan comes together. This is the stage most people underestimate, and it is arguably the most important. Every hour invested in pre-production saves multiple hours during the shoot and in the edit suite.

### What Happens During Pre-Production

Once the brief is approved, our creative team develops the concept. Depending on the type of video, this may include several deliverables.

**Creative concept and treatment.** A written document that describes the overall creative direction, visual style, tone, narrative structure, and how the video will achieve the objectives outlined in the brief.

**Script or interview questions.** For scripted videos, we write a full script with dialogue, voiceover narration, and visual descriptions. For documentary-style or testimonial videos, we develop a detailed list of interview questions designed to elicit the soundbites we need.

**Storyboard or shot list.** Visual representations of key scenes. Storyboards are illustrated frame-by-frame plans, while shot lists are detailed written descriptions of each camera setup. The complexity of the video determines which format is appropriate.

**Location scouting.** Our team visits potential filming locations to assess lighting conditions, acoustics, background aesthetics, power availability, and any logistical challenges. For Bangkok-based shoots, we draw on our extensive knowledge of the city to recommend locations that elevate the production value.

**Casting and talent coordination.** If the video requires on-camera talent, presenters, or voice-over artists, we handle the casting process, including auditions where necessary.

**Production schedule.** A detailed minute-by-minute plan for the shoot day that accounts for setup time, lighting changes, talent availability, meal breaks, and contingency time for unexpected delays.

**Equipment planning.** Based on the creative requirements, we determine the camera systems, lenses, lighting rigs, audio equipment, and any specialty gear such as drones, gimbals, or teleprompters that the project demands.

### What You Should Expect

You will receive each of these documents for review and approval. We encourage thorough feedback at this stage because changes on paper are free, while changes on set cost time and money. Expect at least one or two rounds of revisions on the concept and script before everything is locked down.

### Tips to Make This Stage Smoother

- **Review documents promptly.** Pre-production has the most dependencies, meaning delays at this stage cascade through the entire project timeline.
- **Involve your subject matter experts.** If the video covers technical content, have your experts review the script for accuracy. It is much easier to fix a factual error in a Word document than to reshoot a scene.
- **Confirm your talent availability early.** If specific people from your organization need to appear on camera, lock in their calendars as soon as the shoot dates are proposed.
- **Trust the creative process.** You hired a professional team for a reason. Be open to creative suggestions that might differ from your initial vision. Often, the best ideas emerge through collaborative exploration.
- **Provide all brand assets.** Logos, fonts, color codes, brand guidelines, and any approved music or sound design references should be shared during pre-production so the team can plan accordingly.

## Stage 3: Production Day

**Typical timeline: 1 to 3 days (depending on project scope)**

This is the day everything comes to life. The cameras roll, the lights go up, and all of that careful planning translates into footage. Production days are exciting but also the most resource-intensive part of the process, which is why thorough pre-production matters so much.

### What Happens on Production Day

A typical production day follows a structured schedule designed to maximize efficiency and minimize stress for everyone involved.

**Setup and technical preparation.** The crew arrives before the scheduled call time to set up cameras, lighting, and audio equipment. This can take anywhere from 30 minutes for a simple interview setup to several hours for a complex multi-camera scene with extensive lighting.

**Blocking and rehearsals.** Before recording, we walk through each scene or interview setup to fine-tune framing, lighting, and talent positioning. This is where the storyboard meets reality, and small adjustments are made to optimize the visual composition.

**Principal photography.** This is the main recording phase. Depending on the video type, this could involve conducting interviews, filming scripted scenes, capturing B-roll footage of your facilities or products, or recording live events. Our director manages the creative flow while the crew handles technical execution.

**B-roll and cutaway footage.** In addition to primary content, we capture supplementary footage that adds visual richness to the final edit. This includes detail shots, environmental footage, behind-the-scenes moments, and transitional imagery that supports the narrative.

**Audio recording.** Professional audio is captured simultaneously, whether that means boom microphones for dialogue, lavalier mics for interviews, or ambient sound recording for atmospheric footage.

**Data management.** Throughout the day, our digital imaging technician backs up all footage to multiple drives. We take data security seriously because footage is irreplaceable.

### What You Should Expect

On shoot day, you should expect a professional, organized set where the crew knows exactly what needs to happen and when. If you choose to attend the shoot, you will have the opportunity to review takes on a monitor and provide input. However, we recommend designating one person from your team as the on-set point of contact to streamline communication.

At the end of the shoot day, the crew will pack down equipment and secure all footage. You will not receive raw footage at this point as it goes directly to our post-production team.

### Tips to Make This Stage Smoother

- **Arrive on time.** Production schedules are tight. If talent or key stakeholders are late, the entire day can shift, potentially resulting in rushed shots or missed scenes toward the end of the day.
- **Minimize last-minute changes.** The production day is not the time to overhaul the script or add entirely new scenes. Small adjustments are fine and expected, but significant changes create problems.
- **Prepare your location.** If we are filming at your office or facility, make sure the space is clean, decluttered, and available for the duration of the shoot. Communicate with your team so there are no surprises about noise or access.
- **Keep on-camera talent comfortable.** Nerves are natural. Encourage your team members to be themselves, speak naturally, and not worry about perfection. Our director is experienced at putting people at ease and drawing out authentic performances.
- **Have wardrobe options available.** Even if you discussed wardrobe during pre-production, bringing backup options gives us flexibility. Avoid small patterns, all-white, or all-black clothing, and skip anything with visible logos unless intentional.
- **Provide sustenance.** If your team is involved in a full-day shoot, ensure food and drinks are available. Well-fed talent and crew perform better.

## Stage 4: Post-Production

**Typical timeline: 5 to 15 days**

Post-production is where the magic happens. Raw footage is transformed into a polished, compelling video through a series of specialized processes. This stage often takes longer than clients expect, but the time invested here is what separates professional content from amateur footage.

### What Happens During Post-Production

Post-production is a multi-layered process, and each layer builds on the one before it.

**Footage review and logging.** Before editing begins, our post-production team reviews all captured footage, logs the best takes, transcribes interviews, and organizes everything into a structured project file. For a full production day, this can mean reviewing several hours of raw footage.

**Rough cut assembly.** The editor creates an initial assembly of the video that establishes the narrative structure, pacing, and overall flow. This first version focuses on story and content rather than polish. Music is temporarily placed, and graphics are indicated but not finalized.

**Fine cut editing.** Based on feedback from the rough cut review, the editor refines the pacing, tightens transitions, adjusts the narrative flow, and begins integrating more polished elements. This is where the video really starts to take shape.

**Color grading.** A colorist adjusts the visual tone of the footage to create a cohesive, cinematic look. This includes correcting any inconsistencies in exposure or white balance across different shots and applying a creative grade that matches the desired aesthetic.

**Sound design and audio mixing.** Audio elements are cleaned, balanced, and enhanced. This includes removing background noise from interviews, adding sound effects where appropriate, mixing music levels against dialogue, and ensuring consistent audio quality throughout.

**Music selection and licensing.** If the project requires licensed music, we source tracks from professional music libraries that match the mood and pacing of the video. All licensing is handled on your behalf, ensuring you have full rights to use the music across your intended distribution channels.

**Motion graphics and titles.** Any on-screen text, lower thirds, animated logos, data visualizations, or motion graphics are designed and integrated. These elements are typically created to match your brand guidelines and add a professional polish to the final product.

**Voiceover recording.** If the video includes narration, voiceover is recorded at this stage using either your preferred talent or a professional voice artist we recommend. The recording is then edited and mixed into the video.

### What You Should Expect

You will receive the rough cut for your first round of feedback. This is the most important review stage because structural and content changes are easiest to make at this point. After your feedback, we refine the edit and deliver a fine cut for a second review, followed by a final polish based on any remaining notes.

Most projects include two to three rounds of revisions. We provide clear timelines for each review round and ask that feedback be consolidated from all stakeholders into a single document to avoid conflicting notes.

### Tips to Make This Stage Smoother

- **Consolidate your feedback.** Gather input from all reviewers and send one unified set of notes. Contradictory feedback from different stakeholders is the number one cause of delays in post-production.
- **Be specific with revision requests.** Instead of saying "this section feels off," try "the pacing in the interview segment at 1:23 feels slow. Can we tighten it?" Timecodes and specific descriptions help the editor work efficiently.
- **Review on the right device.** Watch the edit on a proper screen with good speakers or headphones, not on your phone in a noisy cafe. Audio and visual nuances matter.
- **Respect the revision structure.** Save your detailed nitpicks for later rounds and focus the first review on big-picture items like story structure, messaging, and content selection. Polishing a section that might be removed entirely wastes everyone's time.
- **Provide timely feedback.** Post-production workflows have momentum. If the editor is waiting days for your notes, the project loses priority as other work fills the gap, and the overall timeline stretches.

## Stage 5: Review and Delivery

**Typical timeline: 2 to 5 days**

The final stage is all about ensuring the finished video meets every requirement and is delivered in the formats you need for your distribution strategy.

### What Happens During Review and Delivery

**Final quality check.** Before delivery, our team performs a comprehensive technical review. We check for audio levels, color consistency, text accuracy, transition smoothness, and format compliance. Every second of the video is scrutinized to ensure it meets our professional standards.

**Format exports.** Different platforms require different specifications. A video destined for YouTube has different requirements than one for Instagram, LinkedIn, a website hero banner, or a conference presentation. We export your video in all the formats you need, optimized for each platform's specifications.

**Deliverable packaging.** Along with the final video files, we may deliver additional assets depending on your package. These can include still frames from the video for social media or press use, behind-the-scenes photos, subtitled versions, shortened cutdowns for social media, and vertical reformats for mobile-first platforms.

**File transfer.** Final files are delivered via a secure cloud-based transfer system. For large files or multiple deliverables, we use dedicated file transfer platforms that ensure fast, reliable downloads.

**Project archival.** We archive all project files, including raw footage, project files, and final exports, for an agreed period. This means if you need additional edits, reformats, or new versions in the future, we can pick up where we left off without starting from scratch.

### What You Should Expect

You will receive a final review link before we export the full-resolution deliverables. This is your last chance to catch any issues before the video is considered complete. Once you provide final approval, we proceed with the full export and delivery process.

After delivery, we are always available to discuss the video's performance and plan future content. Many of our clients come back for ongoing video production partnerships, and we love building long-term relationships. If you have questions about getting started, our team is always happy to chat. You can reach us through our [contact page](/contact/).

### Tips to Make This Stage Smoother

- **Confirm your delivery specs early.** Let us know all the platforms and formats you need before we begin the final export process. Adding formats after delivery is possible but takes additional time.
- **Proofread all text.** Double-check any on-screen text, titles, and subtitles for accuracy. Typos in a finished video are painful to fix and easy to prevent.
- **Plan your launch.** Coordinate your video release with your marketing team. Having a distribution plan in place before the video is delivered means you can hit the ground running.
- **Provide feedback on the experience.** Let your production team know what worked well and what could be improved. This helps us refine our process and serves you better on future projects.

## Typical Project Timeline: A Summary

Here is a consolidated overview of the timeline for a standard video production project.

| Stage | Typical Duration | Key Deliverables |
|---|---|---|
| Discovery and Brief | 1 to 5 days | Project brief, initial estimate |
| Concept and Pre-Production | 3 to 10 days | Script, storyboard, shot list, schedule |
| Production Day | 1 to 3 days | Raw footage and audio |
| Post-Production | 5 to 15 days | Rough cut, fine cut, final edit |
| Review and Delivery | 2 to 5 days | Final exports in all required formats |

**Total estimated timeline: 2 to 6 weeks for most projects.**

Keep in mind that these are general ranges. Simple projects with decisive feedback cycles can be completed faster, while complex productions with multiple stakeholders or locations may take longer. We provide a detailed timeline specific to your project during the discovery stage.
